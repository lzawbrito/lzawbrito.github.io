# Dance as Weapons
## Civil Simulacra and the Human Material
`08/11/2021`

In the pantheon of history’s greatest rebuttals, few can claim as much fame as
Karl Marx’s treatment of fellow Young Hegelian Bruno Bauer’s writings on the
emancipation of the European Jews in the 19th century—at the time an ongoing
process which evidently perplexed the politics of thinkers such as the Young
Hegelians. His response—the unfortunately titled _On the Jewish Question_—used the
eponymous political issue to launch into a dissection of liberty or emancipation
as an absolute as opposed to emancipation as it relates to a governing body, as
well as the process of abstracting the desire for absolute, material
emancipation into an immaterial, state-dependent desire. These ideas resemble a
specter of later writings of Marx’s—writings which describe the alienation of
labor as a similar dialectical process and propose the infamous economic system
of Communism as an inevitable conclusion to these processes—and such as are
heralded as essential components to Marx’s thought. Indeed, Bauer’s discussion
of Jewish emancipation as relative to the state and Christian emancipation
exemplifies quite well the antithesis of Marx’s thought—that such structures are
independent of material, physical, spatial conditions, and that one is thus
perfectly capable of handling notions of “states” without treatment of more
primitive substances. _On the Jewish Question_ then becomes a resounding negation
of Bauer’s stance, an assertion that, universally, human liberty is a different
and more profound type of liberty than civil liberty. With that, much like how
Marx engages with Bauer to produce these claims, I will engage with Marx to
further develop his theory of liberty and propose that the advocacy for civil
liberty must in fact be annihilated in order for the truer human liberty to be
actualized, for the existence of a civil liberty in fact perpetuates existing
civil states that oppose human emancipation. 

Put briefly, Bauer’s thesis is that the Jewish cause is but one facet of a
larger cause which Jews and Christians alike have stake in: the eradication of
all forms of religious discrimination through the secularization of the state.
Marx characterizes this argument as akin to the sentiment “we have to emancipate
ourselves before we can emancipate others” (28). This, he believes, is to
support Bauer’s opinion that the resolution of the “Jewish question”
necessitates Jewish citizens “cease to be Jewish,” as a completely secular state
must necessarily disallow the practice of religion: “the political abolition of
religion,” Marx says, “is the abolition of all religion” (29). In other words,
Marx is under the impression that, to Bauer, the eradication of strata within a
given facet of civil life requires the eradication of that facet itself, perhaps
because that facet is naturally self-stratifying or because stratification is an
immutable human tendency. Then it follows that some force must actively oppose
the free practice of religion to ensure the liberty from stratification that
Bruno characterizes the Jewish constituency to be demanding—and similarly for
other emancipation movements. The scope of this opposition, of course, demands
that the force be legislative, hence the unavoidable inclusion of the state in
Bauer’s reply to the Jewish question. 

This is where Marx begins to take issue with Bauer. In proposing that the
solution to Jewish emancipation is founded on legislation (or, one could argue,
in proposing the solution should at all be constructed from the state), Bauer
has neglected a different, wholly novel flavor of liberation, much to the
detriment of his “solution.” According to Marx, the colloquial notion of
emancipation is underdefined, and there in fact exists “_political_ emancipation”
and “_human_ emancipation” the “relation between” which he claims is unexamined by
Bauer (30). The emancipation which Bauer treats is the former: emancipation
manifested as legislation, emancipation overseen and carried out by the state,
often an _active_ opposition to the construct from which the populace is
emancipated. Marx would like the _latter_ form of emancipation to be discussed: a
form Marx defines as the negation of political emancipation, a form that is
non-relative, independent of the state, and suggested to be the most desirable.
Marx writes that “to be _politically_ emancipated from religion is not to be
finally and completely emancipated from religion, because political emancipation
is not the final and absolute form of human emancipation.” Instead, political
emancipation constitutes the proclamation of sovereign agnosticism toward social
categories: “[the state] proclaims… that every member of society is an _equal_
partner in popular sovereignty” (32-33). This is to say that political liberty
is a liberty written in the vocabulary of the state and contained only in the
text of the state: it is a symbolic liberty, embalmed and immutable, existing in
the simulacrum of legislation as opposed to in physical space.  Human
emancipation, meanwhile, is the physical liberty that political emancipation
simulates, marked by the destruction of physical stratification—spatial
separation, material possessions, objects-of-labor. Indeed, Marx’s
prioritization of human emancipation conforms to the materialist theory he would
later develop: the categorization of social functions such as religion may be
nominally legislative—or even ideological or cultural—but its origin and
substance is material, spatial, and physical, not intellectual. The ideological
simulacrum of these social stratifications can then—to borrow Marx’s
terminology—be considered a _superstructure_ of material arrangements, emerging
from the material in a form that reinforces and perpetuates physical conditions.

At the interface of the superstructure of politics and the base material
substance lies the economy—in particular, the status of property ownership in a
particular state guides the formation of the policy, ideology, and culture which
we have argued work to preserve the base. Indeed, Marx claims that despite labor
underpinning political economy, “to labor [political economy] gives nothing, and
to private property everything.” The underlying mechanism Marx attributes this
paradox to is his famous “labor alienation” process, of which he claims private
property is a “necessary consequence” (79). He further contends that the
alienation of labor is at the source of every condition from which humanity
could seek liberty—“the emancipation of the workers contains universal human
emancipation,” he writes, “because the whole of human servitude is involved in
the relation of the worker to production”—and as such one can characterize the
purer “human” liberty as a process targeting important _physical_ modes of
exploitation such as labor alienation (79-80). In fact, one sees that the
aforementioned political simulacra in fact _emerge from_ labor alienation, for it
is precisely the process which replaces an object with its specter—material
objects with, to use Baudrillardian terms, hyperreal representations of the
production: say, wage. With humans themselves existing in physicality and not in
any hyperreality, and with the entirety of individuals’ physical consequences on
the world thereby substituted with virtual objects, this becomes a process of,
in Marx’s words, “estranging from man nature _and himself_.” This estranged
self-image is precisely the “citizen” in Marx’s “citizen”-“living individual”
dialectic: the subject of political emancipation, which lives in the text of the
sovereign and is spawned by labor alienation (75, 34).

This is all to strengthen the power of Marx’s communist economic design: the
model seeks to unseat the existing superstructural policies and cultures—a
“_positive_ expression of annulled private property,” he says—in order to
redistribute the product of labor to workers and, given Marx’s labor-centric
characterization of oppression, purportedly secure universal human emancipation.
Marx intends communism as “the _positive_ transcendence of _private property_, or
_human self-estrangement_,” and “the true resolution of the strife between
existence and essence”—suggesting that indeed this program would eventually
reunify the “citizen” with the “living individual,” producing conditions
necessary for the supposed absolute and final human emancipation to be realized
(84). This is all framed, of course, as a process that orbits labor-laborer
unification—the additional features of this utopia are simply corollaries. Yet
Marx’s formulation of the political and human lives suggests an opposing force
to the realization of communism as he wills it: I suggest that if one is to
consider the citizen-living individual dichotomy with gravity, they must admit
that the continued existence of this virtual, estranged self in fact not only
complicates the strategy of emancipation, but also effaces the real self which
is the subject of _human_ emancipation. Marx’s proposed transition _into_ a
communist economy ostensibly takes this obstacle into account: “the Communists,”
the _Communist Manifesto_ reads, “openly declare their ends can be attained only
by the _forcible_ overthrow of all existing social conditions.” For this effort to
be effective, there must be class-wide cooperation so that the workers’ produce,
essential to the livelihood of the bourgeoisie, is effectively held for ransom;
according to Marx, increasingly decadent working conditions and shrinking,
densely-populated working environments such as factories will catalyze this
ideological unity. Yet I argue that Marx has overseen strong sources of divide
within the proletariat, namely the effacing property of the political
simulacrum: the working-class constituency will disagree not only on methods to
use to achieve their cause, but also on what the form of that cause is (in
particular, the form of the oppressor) as well as, in more extreme cases,
whether that cause exists at all. They unequivocally share a true, material,
physical oppressor—and this is the actual object of their struggle—but the
estrangement of the proletariat’s “citizen self” from the material self erases
the marks of this physical oppressor and replaces them with simulated, virtual
oppressors. Emancipatory discourse among the proletariat then becomes a skirmish
of simulated, performative ideologies taking place in a non-physical and
abstract arena. Indeed, this might be a literally _virtual_ setting—I would be
amiss to not mention the degree to which 21st century communication technology
has exacerbated this situation, creating self-reinforcing ideological
information loops, catalyzing radical thought, rendering irony and gravity
indistinguishable—yet this is only the most tangible iteration of a process of
political abstraction that has been long in development. 

Physical proximity is insufficient to engender a coherent proletarian praxis
capable of material action. The further requirement is the annihilation of the
immaterial political personas that the proletariat has been conditioned to fight
for emancipation through: the “citizens.” This may occur naturally—perhaps a
cataclysmic breaking point will be reached in the increasingly splintering and
detached virtual politics, or perhaps environmental deterioration will force the
working masses into a violent confrontation with physicality—or it may require
deliberate instigation from within the political simulacrum. Either way, the
process cannot begin without a break out of illusory emancipatory gestures, for
otherwise the proletariat will continue to live in their “citizen” projection,
not only seeking immaterial victories but also believing that immaterial
gestures are material ones, that superstructural politico-economic complexes are
the essence of arrangements of matter and not the converse. 

This is not to discredit the Marx’s thought: the high spatial density of
laborers was indeed a novel feature of industrial society at the time of Marx’s
conception. However, not only has history shown this was a condition
insufficient for catalysis, but this is a condition that is gradually
dissipating, giving way to low-density intellectual workspaces, work-from-home
models, and “millennialized” idyllic workspaces (see the campuses of the
technology oligopoly). Over this, the youth’s political selves—for which the
internet has become a proxy—have mutated beyond recognition, yielding a thousand
hyper-niche ideologies each irreconcilably incompatible with other another,
further obfuscating the shared material struggle underpinning their ideological
angst. Together, these conditions suggest that the only effective approach to
human emancipation is one which entirely _rejects_ political emancipation, one
which embraces physicality, embraces the material of the human—which we have
seen, capital is willing to put at stake!—an approach which releases the
bustling potential energy of immaterial and virtual political discourse into a
concentrated kinetic strike.
