# In Vitro Neural Networks
## Reflecting on Science's "Moral Responsibilities"
`11/22/2022`

> _I'm very optimistic about what we can do._
> _I’m not very optimistic about what we’ll use it for._ — Rakan Alomran

I recently saw mention of a laboratory realization of an _in vitro_ neural 
network—a neural network in the typical machine learning sense, only assembled 
with _biological_ neurons. Although I unfortunately cannot find the original 
article, I think even in the case that I hallucinated it, it's something worth 
pondering if only as a hypothetical.

Artificial intelligence in its most common, silicon form already causes enough 
friction in our present understanding of consciousness and morality; the advent
of AI cast in the same material as our very own consciousnesses (assuming
materialism) manages to be even more shocking at face value. Or at least 
that was some of my friends' as well as my own first reaction to the headline.
Why? Abstracting away the actual material being used, the purely "informational"
content of an _in vitro_ neural network is not very different from that of a 
machine neural network. If you're concerned about the possible emergent 
consciousnesses of these devices, I think pausing to think about it might reveal 
that there actually isn't much new to be concerned about at all. I really have
no idea what I'm talking about—this is very far from my field—so I could be
wrong about this. But for the sake of discussion let's assume I'm right (lmao).
The _prima facie_ spookiness of the "brain in a vat" neural network (as opposed 
to the familiar, machine one) then must originate in the actual material being 
used to make the artificial intelligence.

What about _that_ is scary? Here's a thought: what is really scary is our
newfound ability to wrangle away that which has been heretofore reserved for
"Mother Nature"—natural processes, unmediated by people. (Natural) disease is
scary, but in a different way that, say, a bio-weapon is; naturally occurring 
genetic mutation doesn't strike me as uncanny as the science fiction laboratory
mutant trope. In the natural case, there is a degree of randomness at play,
randomness which in a sense facilitates superstition. It's not unlike the
ancient Roman practice of leaving unwanted children unattended in the wilderness
and claiming that "nature" is responsible for the child's very likely demise. We
possibly still deify nature like we did then; nature operates according to
principles which are beyond our control—evolution is not scary, because the
stochastic nature of the process allows it to operate "mystically" within the
boundaries of science. The caveat is, of course, that science in fact enables
the very obtaining of said control—this is the flow of knowledge from science to
_engineering_.  

Nature, being "mystical" or stochastic, is free from (naive, intuitive) moral
judgement. Humans, of course, are not. Taking control of the material of nature 
brings with it the baggage of morality—suddenly, nature is not morally
valueless, it's not random, but intentional. In particular, as we might see much
more clearly in the next few years, _biology_ is not valueless. It is my view
that we run into trouble when we forget this.

I have considered whether this is the underlying psychological reason for the 
unique discomfort _in vitro_ NNs, or whether this is just a rationalization of 
that fear. As it stands, I think it is both.

My friend Semir and I recently had a discussion about making moral assessments 
in academia, somewhat in the spirit of Grothendieck. The general conclusion was 
roughly that although no one can feasibly predict or control what the
applications of their research will be and different fields bear 
consequences of varying moral value, it is likely (if not inevitable?) that
among the applications of any field, there will be morally bankrupt ones (I am
pulling from memory here; Semir, if you're reading this, sorry if I'm
misrepresenting). 

How do we control for this? Do we limit access to our publications? Do we just
stop researching? Do we look into increasingly useless questions? (Actually,
sorry G. H. Hardy, but I'm not sure such questions exist at all.) I don't know;
and, at any rate, most human beings lack the luxury of this deliberation. It's
delusional to let yourself be _paralyzed_ by questions like this: for most
people, it is somewhat arrogant to think that your work will have such
measurable consequence. Even then, the world would come to a halt if everyone
made every decision with the butterfly effect in mind, and there are enough
realizations of this trope in media that I don't feel the need to say much more.
My current view, pragmatic and absolving as it may be, is that the future is
determined by the combined actions of a large enough number of agents that my
simulation of quantum many body scars won't lead to nuclear holocaust.  But I
suppose if I was a nuclear physicist running a simulation for the military, it
might.
