# Ignorance, Error, and Description
## On Kripke's Arguments Against Description Theory
`11/21/2022`

Saul Kripke, positioned against the description theory of reference (or,
as it is also known, the cluster theory) spends much of his second
lecture in the *Naming and Necessity* series targeting the following two
core theses of the description theory: (2) "one of the properties \[in
the cluster a speaker believes to correspond to an expression\], or some
conjointly, are believed by \[the speaker\] to pick out some individual
uniquely," and (3) "if most, or a weighted most, of the \[properties\]
are satisfied by one unique object $\gamma$, then $\gamma$ is the referent of
\[the expression\]" (Saul Kripke, p. 71).^[Page references not otherwise marked
are to Kripke's *Naming and Necessity*.] He does so by means of two arguments,
reserved for theses (2) and (3) respectively,
for which I will borrow the terminology "argument from ignorance" and
"argument from error."^[Due to Richard Kimberly Heck.] In what follows, I will argue that these are
indeed distinct arguments, and that both are needed to make a strong
case against the description theory, but that the argument from
ignorance is more susceptible to counterattack by description theorists
than the argument from error.

Kripke begins with the argument from ignorance against thesis (2). The
argument from inference challenges the view that expressions that refer
are associated with descriptions precise enough to *uniquely* pick out
an object, emphasizing the uniqueness requirement. Kripke contends that
there are expressions which *do refer* but whose reference is fixed
(under the description theory) by descriptions too ambiguous to select
an object uniquely; in other words, he looks for an expression that
fails to refer according to thesis (2), but which does refer according
to intuition or common sense or in another demonstrable sense. If he is
successful, the description theory makes an incorrect prediction. To
this end, he selects two examples which he explores in parallel: that of
Cicero and that of Richard Feynman. He says that it is reasonable to
think that those names stand for descriptions of the sort "the
individual who did such-and-such," where such-and-such constitutes some
eminent, representative or otherwise characteristic achievement or
accolade of that person. This model is not hard to justify: it is not
hard to imagine an individual who is not well versed enough in ancient
history to name a property of Cicero other than that he was an ancient
Roman, or that he purportedly wrote *Lorem ipsum*, so on, although it
requires ignorance of, say, what Cicero looked like, or other sense-data
which can serve as unique properties (this point will bring us to a
discussion of acquaintance later). Namely, Kripke says: "Cicero" is
fixed by "the individual who was 'a famous Roman orator,' " "Richard
Feynman" is fixed by "the individual who is 'a physicist or something' "
(*ibid.*, p. 81). A third example, one which appeals less to fame, may
be "John is the individual who does maintenance at my apartment
complex."

These are examples of expressions fixed by descriptions too vague to
uniquely select a reference: there are many famous Roman orators, my
building is maintained by a whole cast of different individuals, and the
description "a physicist *or something*," I hope, speaks for itself;
presumably the speaker is *ignorant* of further, uniquely identifying
descriptions, hence the terminology. But these are also referents which,
according to common sense (or, Kripke's common sense, and the author's
in the John case) *do refer*, even in the case of an ignorant speaker.
There are names, Kripke admits, that are *constructed* by the
description theory (Jack the Ripper is whoever committed those murders,
St. Anne is whoever birthed the virgin Mary) and thereby skirt this
criticism, but Feynman, Cicero, and John are not such names, for it is
nonsensical to append "Richard Feynman, *whoever that may be*." Of
course, if you are a description theorist, you might say: actually,
utterances coming from ignorant speakers *do not refer*, even if the
speaker believes the contrary.

Kripke then turns to the argument from error against thesis (3). Now he
attacks the notion that, taking uniqueness for granted, the object
satisfying the description associated with an expression is indeed the
reference of that expression. This is where he conjures up the famous
Gödel-Schmidt example, briefly reproduced here for thoroughness: The
name "Gödel" is fixed as "the individual who discovered the
incompleteness theorems," but it turns out Kurt Gödel was not the
discoverer of said theorems (perhaps just the publisher) and some other
man Schmidt actually made the discovery. By the description theory,
"Gödel" in fact refers to Schmidt (in the sense of a semantic reference)
but, to Kripke, common sense/intuition has it that "Gödel" still refers
to Kurt Gödel.^[Although in some circumstances, it might be used in a "St. Anne"
fashion; such cases employ speaker's reference.] Kripke's reasons for believing
this are admittedly a bit mysterious, but they are elucidated by critique of his
claim. One reply is: the only circumstance in which someone fixes the name
"Gödel" according to the description Kripke gives (i.e., the association with
the theorems) is one where the speaker is not aware of sufficient facts
associated with the name "Gödel" to use the name correctly in the first
place. Kripke might say in defense: look, even when such an ignorant
speaker uses "Gödel," those who are privy to more specific knowledge
about Kurt Gödel---perhaps just enough knowledge to distinguish Gödel as
an individual possessing qualities other than having purportedly
discovered the incompleteness theorems---still associate the name
"Gödel" with the correct man, even if it is uttered by an ignorant
speaker. If the description theory is correct (more specifically,
applies to anything more than an idiolect) the non-ignorant listener
would in fact picture *Schmidt*.

For the sake of further illustration, let's take a rather extreme
example: as a child, I once had a dream that, following a nondescript
medical procedure, my father was replaced by an identical clone. Perhaps
he differed only in the number of hairs in his head, or some other
nearly imperceptible regard. I would like to think that I know my own
father well enough to refer to him by his name, "Harley," without having
to worry about possessing enough knowledge about him to form a set of
descriptions that might be erroneous in the Gödel-Schmidt sense. In this
scenario, when I use the name "Harley," I do not refer to the the man
living in my house pretending to be my father, I refer to my actual
father, who is elsewhere. If I was a description theorist, I would be
unable to express concern over the disappearance of my father in the
first place: this clone, by construction, fits every description that I
could list off (i.e., that consists of facts about my father I can
perceive), and so it would be absurd for me to approach my mother saying
"That's not the real Harley!" as I would only be saying "Harley is not
Harley!" I admit the extremity of this example might spell its
downfall---how can I possibly identify the clone as an impostor when he
is indeed a *clone*? Perhaps I can say that the same supernatural force
that replaced Harley in the first place also endowed me with the
knowledge that the replacement was made. Does that constitute a
"description" of the object which is the reference of "Harley"? I'm not
sure this is immediately the case. In the event that it isn't, we've
just shown that, even in possession of a maximal set of feasibly
attainable descriptions, description theory fails to make the correct
prediction.

I point out that these attacks on description theory are distinct;
neither reduces to the other. For one, as I earlier noted, attempts to
amend the description theory in the face of the argument from error
might appeal to an ignorant speaker in a fashion similar to the ignorant
speaker defense deployed against the argument from error. These are,
indeed, ignorance of the same kind: in both arguments Kripke gives, the
speaker does not know enough about the actual thing that bears that name
(to other speakers) to identify that thing with the name. I believe the
difference lies in the manner in which the description theory breaks
down in the presence of different types of missing descriptions. You may
have a strong enough description to pick out an object uniquely but
erroneously (by missing the appropriate descriptions), thereby
satisfying all the requirements the description theory lists for
referring to something yet still failing to do so in a manner that
matches our understanding of reference in daily usage. Or, you might
have a weak enough description to not pick out any object at all, while
the usage of the name will indeed be interpreted by others to pick out a
particular thing.

Description theorists might respond to these objections by reaching for
new types of descriptions necessary for an expression to make a certain
reference, or otherwise adjusting what counts as an appropriate
description. Under such alterations the argument from error strikes me
as more resilient. For example, the description theorist might double
down on the "St. Anne" quality of the Cicero example and say: no one in
the immediate community does in fact know enough about Cicero to pick
him out uniquely, all we know is that there is a body of work sitting in
some museum attributed to Cicero, and Cicero is, to present humans,
whoever is responsible for those artifacts. Again, the argument from
error is more resilient to this type of counterargument, as one can
append additional conditions for descriptions that narrow referents down
to an arbitrary degree whilst still leaving the theory open to tailored
counter-examples such as Kripke's or, if correct, mine.
