# Sense Without Reference
## (Short Essay for PHIL1860 - Sense and Reference)
`9/30/2022`

> This is the principle of the theory of denoting I wish to
advocate: that denoting phrases never have any meaning in themselves,
but that every proposition in whose verbal expression they occur has a
meaning. (Bertrand Russell, "On Denoting", p. 480)

This passage sees Bertrand Russell crystallizing the theory of meaning
and denoting "On Denoting" puts forward and placing it in opposition to
the theories of meaning of his contemporaries. In particular, as we will
see, he intends to underscore the ultimate effect his theory of denoting
has on the notion of meaning as it appears in denoting phrases; these
characterizing features, he later argues, are precisely what makes his
theory more desirable (in particular, in my reading, *better defined*)
than, say, Gottlob Frege's. The reasoning that yields this result from
Russell's principles of denotation is as follows.

Russell suggests that a denotation is nothing more than a propositional
function, which necessarily means---and this is key---it must be
supplied a variable to attain a meaning. When we make statements
containing the word *the*, there is an implicit quantifier and an
implicit assertion of uniqueness: there exists some thing, and that is
*the* thing, and that thing also satisfies whatever the remainder of the
proposition contains. The denoting phrase constitutes the "that is *the*
thing" portion of our proposition, and can be written formally as, say,
$Fx$; notably, without the quantifier, the propositional function has no
truth value, which, to Russell, signifies meaninglessness. Here one
could investigate whether Russell is equating definite truth value with
meaning, or that at the very least truth value is a necessary but
possibly insufficient condition for meaning, but this matter would take
us too far astray. Particularities aside, this is precisely what Russell
means by "the denoting phrases never have any meaning in themselves." It
is by fitting the denoting phrase in a proposition that we extract a
meaning from denotation: the proposition introduces a quantifier which
supplies an argument which evaluates the truth value of the denoting
phrase. "Verbal expression" then really amounts to "the semantics of the
sentence which represents that underlying proposition," and we come to
the understanding that utterances must stand for propositions about the
denotation in order to set off the aforementioned mechanism which endows
it with meaning.

Through this construction we do away with the Fregean concept of
"sense"; indeed, this is among the aims of Russell's paper. In
particular, we can argue that Russell's view does not support denoting
phrases with different senses but identical references: there is no way
a denoting phrase can have different different senses in isolation if
the denoting phrase has no meaning, and two (otherwise identical)
propositions containing different denoting phrases referencing the same
object must evaluate to the same truth value, thereby bearing the same
meaning. There are a number of reasons one might wish to provide an
improvement or alternative to Frege's theory---for instance, the
under-definition of sense, which is obviously circumvented by
Russel---but Russell fixates chiefly on the issue of "sense without
reference." The claim is that we can make obviously truth-valued
statements about nonexistent objects, thereby constructing an ostensibly
nonsensical, reference-less sentence with a paradoxically definite
meaning; while Russell exemplifies this pathology with "the King of
France is bald"---which is, he says, "plainly false"---this seems to me
a, for the lack of a better term, "vacuously false" statement, and I
actually believe the phenomenon is better illustrated by an example not
dissimilar to Frege's own "least rapidly converging series" case
(Gottlob Frege, "On Sense and Reference", p. 58). E.g., "the largest
even number is not prime" is a true statement about something that does
not exist, modulo concerns about its *a priori* nature. Compare this
with the baldness of the hypothetical French king—"the King of France
is not bald" is as true as "the King of France is bald," but "the
largest even number is prime" is sure to raise eyebrows. We see a clear
difference in truth value between a certain description of the
nonexistent largest even number and the negation of that same
description, suggesting the sentence is indeed endowed with meaning.

Under Russell's theory this is no longer an issue—Russell's version of
this denoting phrase *contains* the proposition that there *is* a
largest even number (and there is only one largest even number, and it
is not prime, so on, but existence is the pivotal operand Russell
introduces). Thus it is the case that any proposition about the largest
even number is false, that sentences about the largest even number
recover their meaning, and that this reasoning generalizes to all
sentences without reference. In fact, Russell's claim is stronger than
it seems: he says that *every* proposition containing a denoting phrase
has a meaning, i.e., we *cannot* construct a meaningless proposition
containing a denoting phrase. Note that—and I believe this is an
important subtlety to elucidate—Russell says every *proposition*
containing a denoting phrase is meaningful, not that every *sentence*
containing a denoting phrase is meaningful. One can imagine he choose
this phrasing to handle the case of a sentence nonsensical due to a part
other than a denoting phrase, under the assumption that propositions
cannot be nonsensical. That is to say, something like: the type of
sentence Russell is considering necessarily expresses a proposition that
would have a definite truth value if we were to, say, switch out the
reference of the denoting phrase for another reference. Edge case such
as the famous "this sentence is false" are easily dismissed as they are
not propositions, as are imperatives such as "find the largest even
number" (although the fact that one can immediately respond to the
latter with "that's impossible!" does bring into question the possible
meaningfulness of non-propositions about nonexistent objects; I sidestep
this matter as I am not familiar with the analysis of non-propositions).

The question of nonexistent references addressed, Russell's theory may
be further applied to additional puzzles and provide a few other
desiderata—or so he argues. One is the issue of identity: tautologies
such as "$x$ is $x$" carry no knowledge, yet provided two different
denotations of the same thing, one can use everyday language to state
$x=x$ in a manner that *is* seemingly charged with knowledge; see
Russell's "Scott was the author of *Waverley*" example in p. 492. This
is one of Frege's primary concerns, and it is arguably the entire
motivation for his introduction of "sense." Russell, seeking to show
that his formulation can supplant Frege's, explains that his deferring
of meaning to a proposition bearing a denotation modifies the semantics
of such an identification such that the proposition is not simply "$x$
is $x$" but instead something richer: by identifying two denoting
phrases we make the proposition that when a variable satisfies one of
those denotations' propositional functions, the variable is identical to
any variable that satisfies the other denotation's propositional
function. This, of course, is not a tautology.

As a closing remark, I mention that Russell's theory of denoting also
serves him by supplementing his classification of knowledge ("Knowledge
by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description"). He infers that his
theory carries consequences for descriptive knowledge: statements about
things with which one is not acquainted are reducible to propositions
the constituents of which are things with which one *is* acquainted. The
mind example—"so-and-so has a mind which has such and such
properties," where we are not acquainted with so-and-so's mind
itself—is taken to mean something like: for all $x$, if $x$ is
so-and-so's mind, $x$ it has such and such properties (p. 493). We are
presumably acquainted with so-and-so's mind and acquainted with the set
of properties, and no explicit mention is made of the mind itself; we
leave that as an argument which is supplied by the quantifier. Crudely
speaking, we need not know the mind—perhaps not even whether the mind
in question exists—but only that if something satisfies the property
of being that mind, it satisfies a number of other properties. Here it
may be fruitful to question how far we need reduce denotations of
objects to be satisfied (by, say, investigating what it means to be
acquainted with a person and breaking this apart into more granular
acquainted objects and repeating the process), but this seems to me a
question of epistemology and lies outside the scope of the preceding
discussion.
